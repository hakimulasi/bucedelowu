Dick Gregory Overview and Wiki

Early Life and Education

Born in St. Louis, Missouri, Dick Gregory‘s early life was marked by poverty and struggle. Despite these challenges, he excelled in athletics during his high school years, particularly in running, which earned him a scholarship to Southern Illinois University.

His education, however, was interrupted by his draft into the US Army, where he first discovered his talent for comedy.

Early Beginnings and Rise to Fame

Dick Gregory’s career in comedy began in the mid-1950s while serving in the military. After being drafted in 1954 and serving at various locations, including Fort Hood, Texas, and Fort Lee, Virginia, Gregory discovered his comedic talent. Post-discharge, he moved to Chicago with aspirations of becoming a professional comedian.

In 1958, he opened the Apex Club nightclub, which unfortunately faced financial difficulties. However, his fortunes changed in 1961 when Hugh Hefner spotted him at the black-owned Roberts Show Bar in Chicago.

Hefner’s recognition led to Gregory working at the Chicago Playboy Club, marking the launch of his career in mainstream comedy.

Comedy Career and Controversy

Gregory’s comedy was groundbreaking, especially for its time, as he tackled issues of racism and social injustice. His performances were not just about making people laugh; they were also about making them think. His style occasionally stirred controversy, particularly among conservative circles.

For instance, the University of Tennessee branded him an extreme racist and revoked his invitation to speak on campus, a decision that was later overturned by a court ruling.

Political Activism and Writing

Gregory’s career was not limited to comedy. He was deeply involved in political activism, particularly in the 1960s. He protested against the Vietnam War, racial injustice, and advocated for animal rights. His activism led to multiple arrests and participation in hunger strikes.

Gregory also made significant contributions as a writer. His book Nigger: An Autobiography by Dick Gregory, published in 1964, has never been out of print.

He was also a political candidate, running against Richard J. Daley for Mayor of Chicago in 1967 and as a write-in candidate for president in the 1968 United States presidential election.

Post-Standup Career and Media Appearances

After his standup career, Gregory continued to influence through various media. He co-hosted with Cathy Hughes on WOL 1450 AM talk radio and appeared regularly on the nationally syndicated Imus in the Morning program.

Health Advocacy and Entrepreneurship

In the later years of his life, Gregory focused on health advocacy. He became a vegetarian and fasting activist, inspired by the philosophy of nonviolence practiced during the Civil Rights Movement.

His book Dick Gregory’s Natural Diet For Folks Who Eat: Cookin’ With Mother Nature outlined his approach to diet and health.

In 1984, he founded Health Enterprises, Inc., a company distributing weight-loss products, and developed the Bahamian Diet Nutritional Drink.

Legacy and Recognition

Gregory’s career spanned over five decades, during which he used his platform not only for entertainment but also for advocacy and social change. His work earned him a place on Comedy Central’s list of the 100 Greatest Stand-ups of all time and a star on the St. Louis Walk of Fame.

Gregory’s legacy is a testament to his multifaceted talents and his commitment to using comedy as a tool for social commentary and change.

Personal Life

Gregory’s personal life was as rich as his professional one. Married to Lillian Smith, they had 11 children. His family life, though private, was an integral part of his identity, often reflected in his work and activism.
